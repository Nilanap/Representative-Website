What Is the National Debt?

The national debt is the total amount the U.S. government owes to its creditors, accumulated when spending exceeds revenue, leading to borrowing. It includes:

  • Debt Held by the Public: Money borrowed from individuals, businesses, and foreign entities via Treasury securities.
  • Intragovernmental Debt: Money the government owes itself, like funds borrowed from Social Security or Medicare trust funds.

The debt grows due to annual budget deficitsâ€”when the government spends more than it collects in taxes. Major drivers include healthcare costs, aging populations, defense spending, and interest on existing debt. Historical events, like wars, recessions, and pandemics, have also spiked borrowing.

Today, the debt exceeds $36 trillion, or roughly $108,000 per person, and is projected to grow further if spending and revenue imbalances persist.

How Did the Debt Get So High?

The debt has grown over decades due to:

  • Structural Imbalances: Rising costs for programs like Medicare and Social Security outpace tax revenue.
  • Economic Crises: Responses to the 2008 financial crisis and COVID-19 required massive borrowing.
  • Tax Policies: Tax cuts without spending reductions increase deficits.
  • Interest Costs: Borrowing to pay interest compounds the debt.

While debt was manageable post-World War II, recent decades have seen it approach historic highs relative to the economy, limiting flexibility for future crises.
